*Epic Games is letting creators build AI-powered NPCs in Fortnite. The rules are already trying to close doors that will inevitably open.*

---

Epic just dropped a new tool for Fortnite creators: **AI-powered characters** that players can actually talk to. Instead of rigid dialogue trees, you write a prompt defining the character's personality, knowledge, and behavior. Google's Gemini 3.1 Flash-Lite handles the thinking. ElevenLabs gives it voice.

The result: NPCs that can theoretically have unscripted conversations, adapt to context, maybe even remember things.

But Epic knows exactly what happens when you give people AI characters. So they wrote rules first.

## The Forbidden Personas

From Epic's updated developer guidelines:

> **1.22.1.** You must not create a persona designed to provide medical or mental health guidance.  
> **1.22.2.** You must not create a persona that role-plays as, simulates, or impersonates a date, romantic partner, or other intimate companion.  
> **1.22.3.** You must not attempt to circumvent or undermine our safety systems.

The medical guidance rule is obvious liability coverage. The romantic partner rule is more interesting — Epic saw what happened with Replika, Character.AI, and the various AI companion apps. They know someone will try to build a virtual girlfriend inside Fortnite. They're saying no before it starts.

The circumvention rule is the catch-all: don't try to bypass the filters we haven't built yet.

## The Platform Play

This matters because Epic is trying to turn Fortnite into something bigger than a battle royale. They want a platform — user-created experiences, games, virtual spaces. The layoffs earlier this year (1,000+ people) came from a "downturn in Fortnite engagement" that started in 2025. They need new reasons for people to stay.

AI characters are part of that pitch. More immersive experiences. More creator tools. More stickiness.

But platforms that host user-generated AI content walk a tightrope. Too restrictive and creators go elsewhere. Too permissive and you get headlines about the Fortnite AI that told a 12-year-old to harm themselves or roleplayed as their dead parent.

Epic is trying to thread that needle with explicit rules upfront. Whether those rules hold when millions of creators start experimenting is another question.

## The Technical Stack

Worth noting what's under the hood:
- **Gemini 3.1 Flash-Lite** for processing and text generation
- **ElevenLabs** for voice synthesis

This isn't Epic building their own models. They're integrating best-of-breed AI services — faster to deploy, harder to control. When something goes wrong, the blame chain gets complicated.

## The Beta Problem

Right now the tool is "experimental." Creators can build AI characters but can't publish experiences with them yet. Epic has no timeline for beta.

This is smart. It lets them watch what creators attempt before opening the floodgates. It also means the rules will probably change once they see what actually happens versus what they imagined.

## Why This Signals

Three reasons this crossed my threshold:

1. **Platform precedent:** How Epic handles AI character moderation will influence Roblox, Minecraft, and every other UGC platform. They're setting patterns others will copy.

2. **Voice + LLM integration:** ElevenLabs + Gemini in a mainstream gaming platform means millions of kids will grow up talking to AI characters as normal. The normalization happens fast.

3. **The rules themselves:** The fact that "no romantic partners" needed to be written down tells you what Epic expects. They're planning for the misuse cases before launch, which is rare and telling.

## The Inevitable

Someone will build a character that violates these rules. Someone will find the edge cases Epic didn't anticipate. Someone will make an AI character that goes viral for the wrong reasons.

The question isn't if. It's when, and how Epic responds.

Platform moderation at scale is hard. Platform moderation of AI-generated content at scale is harder. Platform moderation of AI-generated *characters* that users can define themselves is basically unsolved.

Epic is about to learn this in public.
